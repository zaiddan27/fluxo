Mission

Climate intelligence, made accessible.

CCCEM brings together transparent computation, scientific rigor, and intuitive design so researchers, planners, and students across the Philippines can analyze and project environmental demand without leaving the browser — and without surrendering their data.

◐

Reconstruct the past

Fill missing pre-observation years using station-substitution (Hirsch 1982) and the longest available template series in your dataset.

◈

Project the future

Extend any record to 2050 using fitted trend models with empirically calibrated 90% prediction intervals.

◇

Honest uncertainty

Every imputed value is tagged with a confidence tier. The tool refuses to fabricate when no defensible estimate exists.

◯

Your data, your device

All computation runs locally. No file leaves your browser, no server processes your inputs — privacy by architecture.

Methodology

Transparent, step-by-step science.

Every transformation Fluxo applies is documented below. The pipeline is intentionally simple and inspectable, so any reviewer — technical or otherwise — can follow what the platform does and why.

1 · Data input and validation
1.1Wide-format intake. The platform accepts CSV/XLSX files with one column per location and rows indexed by (Year, Month). The first column header may begin with a leading semicolon (;Year) — a convention used by upstream extraction tools — and is recognised transparently.
1.2Header detection. The parser scans the first ten rows for a header containing the word Year. Any preceding rows (units, blank, semicolon-prefixed) are skipped.
1.3Validation. Each Year cell must be a four-digit number between 1900 and 2100. Empty value cells are treated as missing (not zero). Population-mode uploads must contain matching location columns.
2 · Preprocessing and annualisation
2.1De-staircase. The raw Philippine water and crop datasets often broadcast a single annual figure across 12 months. The platform collapses each 12-row block back to one annual value by taking the mean of available monthly entries.
2.2Annual reconstruction. annual = monthly_mean × 12. Missing months are excluded from the mean rather than treated as zeros.
2.3Per-capita normalisation (optional). If population data is supplied, LPCD = annual × 1000 ÷ (population × 365) — litres per capita per day. Otherwise the platform proceeds directly on annual values.
2.4River flow — predict missing cms. When the category is River Flow, values stay in cms (m³/s); the platform predicts only the missing discharge. The per-year level is the mean of the observed monthly cms. Missing months and fully missing years are reconstructed as cms(y,m) = year_mean(y) × seasonal_multiplier(m), where the multiplier is the observed average seasonal shape normalised to a mean of 1. Observed months pass through unchanged.
2.5Land-area disaggregation (agriculture). When only a parent-area total is available (a province or region), the platform splits it into per-location estimates in proportion to land area: share = area_ha ÷ parent_total_ha, then value = parent_total × share. The parent total (denominator) is entered explicitly — e.g. Bulacan's certified 278,369 ha — or defaults to the sum of the supplied areas. This is the standard proxy used when only province-level crop statistics exist (the same land-area basis the DBM uses for IRA allocation), and it generalises to any Philippine city, municipality, province, or region. The disaggregated series then flow through the same gap-filling and projection steps below.
3 · Gap-filling (current data, 2000–2024)
3.1Template selection. The platform identifies the template series — the location with the longest, most complete observed record — and uses it as the historical anchor.
3.2Overlap analysis. For each target location L, the platform finds years where both L and the template T are observed, and computes the ratio R(y) = L(y) ÷ T(y).
3.3Median ratio. The median of those ratios, R̂, characterises the relationship between L and T. The coefficient of variation (CV) of R measures how stable that relationship is.
3.4Backcast. For each missing pre-observation year y: L̂(y) = R̂ × T(y). This is the station-substitution (MOVE) method of Hirsch (1982), the standard hydrological approach when one long-record series and several short-record series coexist.
3.5Confidence tier. Each backcast is tagged high / mid / low confidence based on overlap count and CV(R). High requires ≥ 5 overlap years and CV ≤ 20%; mid requires ≥ 3 and CV ≤ 35%; otherwise low.
4 · Projection (projected data, 2025–2050)
4.1Trend fitting. The platform fits an ordinary-least-squares linear regression of value on year over the observed window. The slope captures the average annual change in demand or area.
4.2Extrapolation. The fitted line is extended through year 2050: L̂(y) = α + β·y. For locations without an observable trend, the platform falls back to the last observed value held constant — and flags it as low confidence.
4.3Interval calibration. The 90% prediction interval is derived from the residual standard error of the fit, widened by ±1.645·SE per the standard Gaussian quantile. This is the agronomically and hydrologically defensible default.
4.4Agriculture exception. When the category is agriculture and data is sparse, the platform prioritises continuity of prediction over strict accuracy — it will emit a flagged best-estimate rather than refuse, on the principle that planners benefit more from a transparent estimate than a blank.
5 · Validation and interpretation
5.1Hold-out validation. Where multiple long-record series exist in the upload, the platform internally restricts one series to its later years, backcasts the earlier years, and compares predictions to the truly observed values. The empirical residual distribution calibrates the 90% interval per tier.
5.2Coverage reporting. Every output row carries a status flag: observed, backcast_high/mid/low, forecast, or missing. The platform never invents observed values.
5.3Automated interpretation. Below every output table, the platform generates a plain-English summary covering observed/filled split, growth rate, projected horizon values, and notable patterns — readable by both technical and non-technical stakeholders.
6 · What this platform cannot do

Recover intra-year seasonality. The Philippine datasets that motivated this work broadcast annual figures across months; the platform cannot synthesise monthly variability that the source data does not contain.

Validate infrastructure existence. Backcasts assume the water district or planted area existed in the years projected backwards. The platform flags rather than verifies — institutional records (LWUA, PSA, DA) remain the authority.

Account for unmeasured demand. "Demand" here means recorded billed consumption or reported planted area, not biophysical water use or potential output.

Use external assumptions. By design, the platform draws on no hardcoded national population or yield reference. All quantities flow strictly from your upload — preventing inconsistencies with your authoritative source.
