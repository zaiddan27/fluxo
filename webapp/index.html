Bulacan Urban Water Demand Gap-Filling Tool

Central Luzon State University · Nueva Ecija, Philippines

What this tool does

Upload an Excel or CSV file in the same format as the Bulacan urban water-demand records (year, month, municipality columns in cubic meters). The tool will:

  • De-staircase annual values broadcast across 12 months
  • Compute observed per-capita demand (LPCD) using PSA census population
  • Backcast missing pre-observation years using the station-substitution (MOVE) method, with DRT as the long-record template
  • Produce 90% confidence intervals calibrated against held-out validation
  • Report each year with a confidence flag — and refuse to fabricate when no defensible estimate is possible

Methodology

How the gap-filling works (plain English)

The raw data is, surprisingly, not really monthly. All 12 monthly values in each year are identical or near-identical — they represent an annual figure broadcast across 12 months. The tool first collapses each 12-row block back to one annual figure.

Then for each municipality (M) that begins after year 2000, the tool finds the overlap years with Doña Remedios Trinidad (DRT) — the one municipality with a full 2000–2024 record. It computes a ratio

R = LPCDM(y) / LPCDDRT(y)

for each overlap year, takes the median, and uses it to backcast:

LPCDM(y) = median(R) × LPCDDRT(y) for y < first observed year

Annual demand is then LPCD × population × 365 / 1000 and the monthly figure is the annual ÷ 12.

This is the station-substitution / record-extension method codified by Hirsch (1982) [1] and used in WMO Technical Note 175 [3]. It is the standard hydrology approach when one long-record gauge exists and several short-record gauges need their history reconstructed.

How the 90% confidence intervals are calibrated

Naive bootstrap CI on the observed-period ratio under-states uncertainty — it doesn't capture the dominant risk that DRT's per-capita trajectory may not faithfully represent the target municipality's trajectory.

We instead run a hold-out validation: for each municipality whose record begins 2005–2006, we artificially restrict its observed window to 2015+, backcast 2005–2014, and compare to the truly-observed values. From those validation residuals we compute, per confidence tier, the empirical 5th and 95th percentiles of

(true − pred) / pred

and use them as the calibrated CI bounds. This is the standard post-hoc calibration approach in ensemble forecasting [4].

Per-tier 90% predictive intervals:

  • backcast_high (29 validation rows): [−21%, +34%], coverage 86%
  • backcast_mid (40): [−13%, +121%], coverage 90%
  • backcast_low (28): [+15%, +215%], coverage 86%

The asymmetric, positive-skewed bounds for mid/low tiers reflect a real, documented finding: the rural-DRT template under-estimates peri-urban demand (Marilao, Meycauayan, SJDM, etc.). We report this honestly rather than hide it.

Why this method, not ARIMA / MICE / MissForest / Bayesian?

We considered every standard method. None fit this dataset's structure better than MOVE.

  • ARIMA / Kalman (state-space) [2]: needs a long observed signal in the target itself. Most targets only have 9–19 years.
  • MICE / MICE-RF [5]: assumes Missing-At-Random. Our missingness is structurally early-period, not random.
  • MissForest [6]: same MAR assumption issue.
  • Bayesian hierarchical (PyMC / Stan): stronger if we had multiple long-record stations across municipality types. With one long record (DRT, rural), hierarchical pooling adds shrinkage but no new information.
  • Direct Sampling [7]: designed for stochastic intra-year reconstruction. Overkill — our data lacks intra-year variation by construction.
What this tool cannot do

1. It cannot recover monthly seasonality. The source data has none. Monthly output is annual ÷ 12.

2. Peri-urban backcasts are lower bounds. Marilao, Meycauayan, Bulakan, San Jose del Monte, and Obando grew faster than the rural DRT template. Their backcasts are flagged backcast_low and the upper CI bound is set wide.

3. It cannot fill nonexistent infrastructure. Water districts established after 2000 should not be backcast as if they had service in earlier years. We confirmed 4 of 22 districts existed pre-2000 (Norzagaray 1986, Bulakan 1989, Bocaue 1979, Obando 1978). The remaining 18 require verification via LWUA — backcasts for those are marked as model output, not historical record.

4. "Demand" = billed consumption, not actual water use. Pre-meter-coverage demand is a different quantity from billed demand. The backcast projects billed-equivalent demand under a constant-meter-coverage assumption.
